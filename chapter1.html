Chapter 1 of 4 · The Need

Somewhere tonight, a family is starting over with nothing.

A wildfire in the BC Interior. A flood in the Fraser Valley. A factory that automated away the only work a town ever had. The causes differ. The result is the same: people who had a place, and now don't.

Displacement is usually described in numbers — hectares burned, homes lost, jobs eliminated. The numbers are real, but they hide the actual shape of the problem: a person standing on ground that used to be home, with no clear next step, and no institution built to give them one.

Emergency response gets people out. It rarely gets them somewhere. Shelters are temporary by design. Insurance payouts, when they exist at all, cover a structure, not a community. The gap between "rescued" and "rebuilt" is where people are left the longest, and it is largely unaddressed.

"The Oasis does not warehouse the displaced. It invites them home."

This is not only about disaster. Automation displaces workers with no fault of their own. Indigenous communities have lived with land dispossession for generations, a displacement that predates and compounds every other kind. Reintegration with dignity — not shelter alone, but purpose, land, and belonging — is the actual need underneath all of these.
