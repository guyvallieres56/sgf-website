Chapter 2 of 4 · The Systemic Causes

It isn't bad luck. It's a design choice, repeated.

Every displacement in Chapter One has a structural cause underneath it — and none of them are new. What's new is how often they're now converging on the same communities at once.

Settlement on the wrong terms. Development has repeatedly pushed into fire-prone forests, floodplains, and drought-vulnerable land because the land was cheap or available, not because it was safe. The risk was known. It was priced in, or ignored, rather than designed around.

Extraction without replacement. Economies built on burning a finite resource treat the resource as inexhaustible until it very suddenly isn't. What SGF calls the Petroleum Precautionary Principle asks a simple question most planning ignores: what happens after the last barrel, if nothing new was ever built to replace it?

Manufactured scarcity. Housing, energy, and food are frequently withheld or overpriced not because supply is impossible, but because scarcity is more profitable than abundance. A community designed for actual self-sufficiency removes the leverage scarcity depends on.

"You never change things by fighting the existing reality. You build the new that makes it obsolete." — Buckminster Fuller

None of these causes are solved by better emergency response. They are solved, if at all, by not rebuilding the same vulnerable model in the same vulnerable way — which is exactly the fork in the road the next chapter is about.
